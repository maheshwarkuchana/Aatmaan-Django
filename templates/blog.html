Malaria

Malaria is a parasite-stricken disease. The parasite is transmitted to humans through infected mosquito bites. People with malaria generally feel very sick, with high fever, and chills sweating. Around 210 million people become diagnosed with malaria each year, and about 440,000 die from the disease. The majority of the people who die from the disease are young African children.

While the disease is uncommon in temperate climates, malaria continues to be widespread in tropical and subtropical countries. World health officials are seeking to minimize malaria incidence by providing bed nets to help shield people from mosquito bites when they're sleeping. Scientists worldwide are working to create a malaria-preventing vaccine.

If you are traveling to places where malaria is common, take steps by wearing protective clothing, using insect repellants and sleeping under treated mosquito nets to prevent mosquito bites. You may also wish to take preventive medicine before, during and after your trip, depending on the area you are visiting and your individual risk factors for infection. There are now many malaria parasites that are immune to the most common drugs used to treat the disease.

Symptoms

Infection with malaria is usually characterized by first sign/common symptoms of malaria include:

  • Very high temperature(fever)
  • Headache
  • Vomiting
  • Nausea
  • Joint/muscle pain
  • Fatigue
  • Loss of Appetite
  • Profuse Sweating
  • Chest or stomach pain
  • Coughing
  • Sore throat
Many people who meet malaria phases of "attacks" malaria. An attack usually begins with shivering and freezing, accompanied by a high fever, accompanied by vomiting and a return to normal temperature. Signs and symptoms of malaria usually begin within a few weeks after an infected mosquito has bitten them. Some kinds of malaria parasites, however, can lie dormant in your body for up to a year.

Causes

Malaria is caused by a microscopic kind of parasite. The parasite is most frequently transmitted to humans by mosquito bites.

Other modes of transmission
Since malaria-causing parasites damage red blood cells, people may also catch malaria from exposure to infected blood including:

  • from mother to unborn child
  • by sharing needles used to inject drugs
  • Through blood transfusions
